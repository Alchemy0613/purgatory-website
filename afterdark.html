Origin

Why I Do This

Most people grow up wanting to be teachers, nurses, astronauts. I grew up wanting to understand the things nobody else wanted to talk about — why people lie, why people kill, and why small towns bury their secrets deeper than their dead.

I didn’t choose the dark. The dark chose me.

I was the kid in 9th grade building my own encyclopedia of serial killers while everyone else was trading lip gloss and homework answers. Not because I was twisted — because I was curious. Because patterns made sense to me when people didn’t.

I went into criminal justice because I wanted answers. I wanted to be a forensic pathologist — the one who speaks for the dead when the living won’t.

Then I had a daughter, and life rerouted me. I switched to EMT, and when the professor asked why I wanted to be there, I told the truth:

“If I can’t work on the dead ones, I might as well try to save the living.”

She stared. My classmates laughed. She didn’t. So I doubled down:

“I might just get slapped playing with the living’s insides trying to find cause of death.”

She pushed me harder after that. Maybe she saw something in me. Maybe she was scared of it.

Life didn’t slow down. My aunt passed. I got hurt at work. Ten years slipped by. When I finally went back to the field, a week into the refresher course, my dad died in my arms — in front of my daughter — the day before her 13th birthday, the day before he was supposed to retire.

Forty‑five seconds disappeared. Reality ripped open. I couldn’t breathe. I couldn’t think. I couldn’t move. I haven’t slept right since.

But that night didn’t break me. It forged me. It made me understand why I’ve always been drawn to the shadows — not because I love darkness, but because I refuse to look away from it.

Identity

What After‑Dark Is

After‑Dark isn’t a hobby. It’s not a trend. It’s not shock value. It’s the place where all the pieces of me finally make sense.

  • The criminal justice major
  • The almost‑forensic‑pathologist
  • The EMT with the blunt honesty
  • The kid who built a serial killer encyclopedia
  • The woman who sees patterns others ignore
  • The one who grew up around secrets
  • The one who asks the questions nobody else will

This is where I talk about mafia history, serial killers, suspicious deaths, corruption, cold cases, small‑town shadows, and the stories that slipped through the cracks — not to sensationalize, not to accuse, not to glorify, but to understand, to analyze, and to shine a light where nobody else wants to look.
